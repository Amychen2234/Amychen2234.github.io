    2) Tendency to ask many questions without leaving room for other person to reciprocate. Why? Others might show lack of curiosity and a desire to impress others and want to build trust instantly.

  • Internal reflections

    • 1) Recognition that self-trust is a challenge in various aspects of life and acknowledgement of high personal standards leading to pressure and difficulty in meeting them.

    2) Insight into the power of listening to build self-trust and calmness in interactions and acknowledgement to give oneself permission to take ownership and create personal rules.

  • Top practices to work on

    • 1) Self-awareness: keep a journal noting likes/dislikes, feelings during interactions (anxious or calm) and use the journal to analyze and improve my approach to social interactions

    2) Build self-trust: focus on trusting yourself to follow through on intentions or actions and maintain a record of instances where you've trusted yourself well and where improvement is needed. Use these records to enhance consistency and confidence in social interactions.

    3) Balanced conversational approach: during conversations, aim for a balanced exchange of questions and personal sharing and allow space for the other person to ask questions and share thoughts without feeling pressured to lead the conversation. Embrace pauses and allow conversations to unfold naturally, revealing aspects of yourself over time.

    4) Ownership and rule setting: recognize the importance of setting your own rules in social interactions to reduce pressure and enhance authenticity and view the process of improving social skills as an art and a journey towards becoming the person you aspire to be.

  • Challenges in staying focused on one topic and managing negative interactions/boundries.

    • 1) Try to stay on the same topic with guidance by asking questions - a good way to get to know them and their interests and focus on the topic.

    2) Wait for people to answer your questions so that you can ask the next questions - stay on the same topic and keep the same topic for a period of time.

    3) Notice that you sometimes feel nervous and uncertain about whether others are interested in the topic of conversation. Instead of constantly second-guessing their interests, which may lead to overthinking and rapid topic changes, you can practice on mindfullness to focus on what the other person is saying vs letting your mind wander to possible reactions or judgement. This behavior stems from concerns about how others might feel or react, which can distract you from truly listening to and engaging with what others are sharing. After conversations, reflect on what went well and what you might improve. Did you listen actively? Did you stay focused on the topic? Learning from each interaction can help you feel more confident in future conversations.

    4) Can focus on the nature of the conversation vs if moving too quickly and not allowing the person to pause and give them space. Some people may not know how to move the conversations - you may feel it is your responsibility to keep the conversation going. So you are not doing something wrong. Can be one-sided if you don't allow the other person to go deeper into the topic or ask questions.

    5) When dealing with phone calls where others may speak critically or condescendingly, it's crucial to manage your energy. Maintain good posture and stand upright to assert control over the situation; slouching can exacerbate negative feelings. Consider shortening the conversation if it becomes particularly detrimental; a negative interaction can impact your mood for days. If someone makes you feel inferior, use it as an opportunity to strengthen your self-esteem, assert boundaries, and establish autonomy. This approach empowers you to reshape the dynamics of your interactions.

Session 4: 7/11/2024

  • The Art of Conversation: Practice, Reflect, and Connect

    • 1) Engage and Observe: Actively participate in conversations, observing both your interactions and those of others. Notice what makes interactions satisfying and enjoyable.

    2) Identify Disconnects: If you leave a conversation feeling irritated or misunderstood, consider whether it was a disconnect or a mismatch in interests. Reflect on your emotions during and after the conversation to better understand your experience.

    3) Continuous Improvement: View each conversation as an opportunity to grow. There is no right or wrong, just ongoing improvement. Reflect on how the conversation made you feel and what you learned about yourself and the topic.

  • The Art of Conversation: Set Intentions and Transition Smoothly

    • 1) Pre-conversation Preparation: Before engaging, set clear intentions for the interaction. Think about the type of connection you expect and the outcomes you desire. Reflect on questions like: What do I feel? Was I kind? Were they kind to me? Did I listen, and did they listen to me? Did both parties walk away with positive experiences?

    2) Smooth Transitions: Use transitional phrases to shift topics naturally, such as "I have a question about something else," "That reminds me of another topic," or "Out of curiosity, what are your thoughts on this?" These help create bridges to new subjects, maintaining the flow of conversation.

  • The Art of Conversation: Understand and Exchange Value

    • 1) Value Perception: Recognize that different people have different perceptions and values. What you value might be perceived differently by others. Dig deeper to understand these differences and shift perspectives accordingly.

    2) Mutual Value Exchange: When dealing with others, especially younger individuals who may feel inferior, acknowledge what you offer and what they value. Exchange information to fulfill each other’s needs and enhance mutual understanding. For example, if someone values specific information, provide it and seek to understand their perspective.

    3) Meeting Reflections: After meetings, reflect on your experiences to build internal confidence. Write down your feelings, noting what made you feel good and what made you feel insecure. The more you understand yourself, the more confidently you can engage in future conversations. Recognize that you and others are on the same level and learn from how they communicate to improve your own interactions.
